Overtime is one of the most mismanaged aspects of Indian payroll - not because HR managers are careless, but because the rules are genuinely complicated. The Factories Act says one thing. The Shops and Establishments Act in your state may say something different. What applies to a manufacturing worker does not apply to an IT professional. What is mandatory in Tamil Nadu may be discretionary in Delhi. And on top of all this, company policy sometimes diverges from statutory minimums in ways that create their own compliance obligations.

The result is that many Indian HR teams manage overtime reactively: employees submit OT claims, someone approves them, and payroll processes them. There is rarely a system for tracking whether OT has exceeded legal limits, whether the right rate has been applied, or whether the company is exposed to liability for underpayment.

This guide is a practical reference for understanding overtime law in India and building the processes to manage it correctly.

---

## Overtime Under the Factories Act

The Factories Act, 1948 is the primary statute governing overtime for factory workers in India. The key provisions are in Sections 51 through 64.

**Section 51** sets the ordinary working hours at 48 hours per week.

**Section 54** limits the working day to 9 hours (including intervals).

**Section 59** is the core overtime provision. It states that any worker who works more than 9 hours in a day or more than 48 hours in a week is entitled to overtime wages at twice the ordinary rate of wages.

Note the critical phrase: "twice the ordinary rate." This means 2× pay for OT, not 1.5×. Many employers apply a 1.5× rate (the international norm under regulations like the US FLSA), which constitutes underpayment under Indian law.

**Section 64** provides for exemptions - certain workers in supervisory or managerial roles can be excluded from the working hours limit. These exemptions require state government approval in most cases and are narrower than many employers assume.

**Section 61** limits total hours including overtime to 60 hours per week, and no worker can be required to work overtime for more than 50 hours in a quarter (13 weeks). This quarterly cap is widely ignored in manual systems because it requires tracking cumulative OT over multiple pay periods - something that is nearly impossible to do reliably without software.

## Shops and Establishments Acts: State-Level Variations

For employees working outside factories - retail, hospitality, IT, finance, professional services - the relevant law is the Shops and Establishments Act of the respective state. These acts vary significantly.

| State | OT Rate | Weekly Hours Limit | Max Daily OT | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maharashtra | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 3 hours | Shops and Est. Act 2017 applies |
| Karnataka | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 2 hours | IT/ITeS sector has special exemptions |
| Tamil Nadu | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | Not specified | Applies to shops, commercial establishments |
| Delhi | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 2 hours | Delhi Shops & Establishments Act 1954 |
| Telangana | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 2 hours | Amended 2021 provisions apply |
| Haryana | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 2 hours | Factories Act applies to manufacturing |
| Gujarat | 2× ordinary rate | 48 hours | 3 hours | Gujarat Shops Act 2019 |

Most states have converged on 2× pay for overtime hours. However, the definition of "ordinary rate of wages" varies - some states include allowances in the base calculation, others compute only on basic pay. This definitional difference can be significant when employees have substantial variable components in their salary.

## Comp-Off vs Cash Overtime: Choosing the Right Approach

Indian law does not universally mandate cash payment for overtime - in many non-factory contexts, compensatory off (comp-off) is permissible. The choice has implications beyond just payment method.

**Cash overtime** is straightforward: extra hours are paid at the applicable rate in the same month's payroll. It is clean from a compliance standpoint and preferred by employees who need the income.

**Comp-off** grants the employee an additional paid day off for overtime worked. It avoids the immediate payroll cost, but creates a liability that must be tracked. If comp-off is not taken within a defined window (typically the same quarter), it usually needs to be encashed - creating a deferred payroll obligation.

The compliance risk with comp-off is that many companies grant it informally - a verbal "take a day off whenever" - with no documentation. This creates disputes at the time of settlement and leaves the company unable to prove that the OT liability was discharged.

The right approach is to make comp-off a tracked, formal entry in your attendance system: OT hours logged, comp-off granted, leave balance updated, and expiry tracked. When a comp-off is taken, it should be recorded as a specific leave type so that the balance reconciles.

## Industry-Specific Considerations

**Manufacturing** companies are fully governed by the Factories Act for production floor workers. The 2× rate and quarterly caps apply strictly. OT in the last quarter of the financial year is a common compliance risk area - high production demand drives OT, and few companies track whether individual workers are approaching the 50-hour quarterly limit.

**IT and ITeS** companies in certain states have secured exemptions from working hours provisions under their state Shops Act, primarily for software export companies. However, these exemptions do not eliminate the obligation to compensate fairly for long hours - they remove the legal cap, not the company's contractual obligations.

**Retail** is subject to state Shops Acts. Peak periods (Diwali, festive season, sale periods) drive significant OT. Because retail employees often have lower base salaries, the per-hour OT cost is lower in absolute terms, but the volume of untracked OT hours can be very high.

**Healthcare** has unique provisions under some state acts that recognise the nature of round-the-clock operations. OT rates and shift differential rules for nursing staff and paramedics need careful attention.

## How Attendance Software Manages OT Compliance Automatically

Manual overtime management is inherently reactive. The supervisor knows someone worked late, approves the claim, payroll processes it - with no one checking whether the rate was correct or whether the cumulative total is approaching a statutory limit.

Attendance software with an OT management module changes this in several ways.

**Automatic OT flagging**: When an employee's shift ends and they have not checked out, the system continues tracking. When total hours cross the daily or weekly threshold, the excess is automatically classified as overtime.

**Rate application rules**: The system applies the correct rate (2× or applicable rate) based on the employee's job category, state, and establishment type - without manual calculation.

**Cumulative tracking**: The system maintains a running total of OT hours per employee per quarter, and can alert HR when an individual is approaching the statutory cap. This is the single biggest compliance gap in manual systems.

**Manager approval workflow**: OT should be pre-approved where possible, or at minimum, flagged for manager review before it is processed into payroll. Software creates a documented approval trail.

**Comp-off ledger**: Comp-off earned, comp-off taken, and comp-off pending are tracked as a formal balance alongside regular leave, with expiry alerts when unused comp-off is approaching its encashment date.

## Common Compliance Mistakes to Avoid

**Applying 1.5× instead of 2×**: The most frequent error. Indian law requires 2× for most categories of workers. Check your payroll configuration.

**Including only basic pay in OT rate base**: Many states require OT to be calculated on the total ordinary wages including dearness allowance. Check your state's specific Shops Act definition.

**Ignoring the quarterly OT cap**: The 50-hour-per-quarter limit under the Factories Act is widely violated because no one is tracking cumulative hours. Software is the only practical solution here.

**Informal comp-off with no records**: Verbal comp-off arrangements are unenforceable in disputes and create hidden liabilities.

**Blanket exemptions for all managers**: "Managerial" exemptions require the employee to genuinely exercise managerial or supervisory authority and, in most cases, require state government notification. Title alone is not sufficient.
