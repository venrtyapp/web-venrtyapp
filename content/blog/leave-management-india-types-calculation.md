If you have ever tried to explain your company's leave policy to a new employee and watched their eyes glaze over halfway through, you are not alone. Leave management in India is genuinely complex - not because companies make it so deliberately, but because the underlying legal framework layers central legislation on top of state-specific rules, and most organisations add their own policies on top of that.

The result is that even experienced HR professionals sometimes get the rules wrong. Casual leave and earned leave get conflated. Encashment is offered when it should not be, or denied when it should be mandatory. Carryover limits are set informally and then disputed when an employee tries to claim them.

This guide is a practical reference. Whether you are drafting a leave policy from scratch, auditing an existing one, or evaluating whether your current process can survive a labour audit, this post covers what you need to know.

---

## The Core Leave Types Under Indian Law

Indian employees are entitled to several categories of leave, governed by a patchwork of central and state laws. Here is a summary of the most common types:

**Earned Leave (EL) / Privilege Leave (PL)**
Earned leave accrues based on the number of days worked. Under the Factories Act, a worker is entitled to one day of EL for every 20 days of work (for adults). Under the Shops and Establishments Act, the ratio varies by state - in Maharashtra, it is one day for every 22 days worked; in Karnataka, one for every 20. EL is the leave type most commonly subject to encashment and carryover rules.

**Casual Leave (CL)**
Casual leave is typically for short, unplanned absences - personal errands, mild illness, or domestic situations that do not warrant a formal sick leave application. The standard quantum is 7–12 days per year depending on the state and industry. Casual leave generally cannot be carried over to the next year and is not encashable in most states.

**Sick Leave (SL)**
Sick leave is for medical reasons, and many states require a doctor's certificate for absences beyond two or three consecutive days. The quantum under most Shops & Establishments Acts is 7–10 days per year. In some organisations, SL and CL are merged into a single "personal leave" bucket for simplicity, though this can create compliance issues.

**Maternity Leave**
Under the Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017, women employees in establishments with 10 or more employees are entitled to 26 weeks of paid maternity leave for the first two children, and 12 weeks for the third child onwards. Establishments with 50 or more employees must also provide crèche facilities.

**Paternity Leave**
India does not yet have a central law mandating paternity leave for private sector employees. Some state governments and the central government (for its own employees) provide 15 days. Most progressive private sector employers now offer 5–15 days as part of their HR policy, but it is not a statutory requirement across the board.

**Bereavement Leave**
Similarly, bereavement leave is not mandated centrally. Most companies offer 3–5 days for immediate family members. This is governed by company policy, not statute.

**National and Public Holidays**
The Negotiable Instruments Act lists 3 national holidays - Republic Day, Independence Day, and Gandhi Jayanti - on which all establishments must remain closed or provide substituted leave. Regional/festival holidays vary by state; most states have 14–18 listed public holidays per year.

## How Leave Accrual Works: Monthly vs Annual

Leave accrual can be structured in two primary ways:

**Annual credit:** The entire leave entitlement is credited on January 1 (or the start of the leave year, which varies by company). An employee joining mid-year typically receives a pro-rated credit. This is simpler to communicate but creates a risk of employees exhausting leave early in the year.

**Monthly credit:** One-twelfth of the annual entitlement is credited at the start of each month (or at the end, depending on policy). This is more equitable and reduces the risk of over-withdrawal, but requires the HR system to run a monthly credit job and handle joining/exit proration correctly.

For EL specifically, the Factories Act mandates that leave is calculated based on days actually worked in the preceding year - making monthly crediting the more legally accurate approach for factory workers.

## State-Wise Variations Under the Shops and Establishments Act

This is where things get complicated for companies operating across multiple states. The Shops and Establishments Act is a state legislation, and the leave entitlements and rules differ meaningfully:

| State | CL (days/year) | EL accrual basis | CL carryover allowed? | EL carryover limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maharashtra | 8 | 1 per 22 days worked | No | 45 days |
| Karnataka | 12 | 1 per 20 days worked | No | 30 days |
| Delhi | 10 | 1 per 18 days worked | No | 30 days |
| Tamil Nadu | 12 | 1 per 20 days worked | No | 30 days |
| Telangana | 10 | 1 per 20 days worked | No | 30 days |

Note: These figures are indicative and subject to periodic amendment. Always verify against the current version of the relevant state act before finalising your policy.

Companies with offices in three or more states often standardise on the most generous entitlement (for employee benefit) or the stricter minimum (for compliance). The former is better HR practice; the latter creates risk if audited in a state with higher entitlements.

## Leave Encashment Rules

Leave encashment - paying employees for unused leave rather than requiring them to take it - is governed by a combination of law and company policy:

- **At year end:** Encashment of unused EL at year end is not universally mandated, but many companies offer it as an incentive for attendance discipline.
- **At separation:** This is where the law is firm. Under the Factories Act and most Shops & Establishments Acts, employees are entitled to encashment of accumulated EL at the time of resignation, retirement, or termination. The calculation is based on the daily wage rate at the time of exit.
- **Tax treatment:** Leave encashment on retirement from a government job is fully tax-exempt. For private sector employees, encashment at retirement is exempt up to ₹25 lakh (revised from ₹3 lakh in 2023). During employment, any encashment is fully taxable as salary income.

A common mistake is to cap the encashable balance without reflecting this cap in the actual leave ledger - leading to disputes where an employee believes they have 60 EL days but the company's cap means only 45 are payable.

## Carryover Limits and the Problem of Stale Leave Balances

Most states cap how much EL can be carried over from one year to the next (30–45 days is typical). Leave that exceeds this cap at year end is either encashed, lapsed, or donated (some companies have leave donation policies for serious medical situations).

Without automated carryover logic, HR teams typically spend the last week of December manually adjusting dozens or hundreds of leave balance records. Errors are common. Employees who check their balance in January and find it unexpectedly lower than expected will raise tickets - and if HR cannot explain the calculation, trust erodes.

## Tips for Creating a Compliant Leave Policy

1. **Define the leave year clearly.** Is it January to December, April to March, or the employee's anniversary date? Inconsistency causes calculation errors.
2. **Document the accrual method.** Specify whether it is monthly or annual, and how pro-ration works for joiners and exiters.
3. **Set carryover and encashment limits explicitly.** Reference the state law and state your company's policy where it goes beyond the legal minimum.
4. **Create an exceptions workflow.** Employees with serious illness, pregnancy complications, or family emergencies will need exceptions. Have a process for it rather than handling it case-by-case informally.
5. **Communicate the policy at onboarding and annually.** A policy that employees do not know about is a policy that generates disputes.
6. **Audit your balances quarterly.** Catch accrual errors before year-end.
