There is a type of financial loss that does not show up in any audit, does not trigger any alarm, and goes largely undetected until someone decides to look closely. It does not involve stolen cash or manipulated invoices. It involves stolen time - and in most Indian workplaces, it is happening every single day.

Time theft is the practice of employees being paid for hours they did not work. It sounds dramatic when named directly, but most instances of it are mundane: a colleague tapping in for someone who is stuck in traffic, an employee extending lunch by twenty minutes three times a week, a worker checking out slightly later on paper than they actually left. Each individual incident is small. Across a workforce of any size, they add up quickly.

This is not primarily a morale issue or a character judgment on your employees. It is a systems problem - and like most systems problems, it has a systems solution.

---

## What Time Theft Actually Looks Like

Understanding the specific forms time theft takes helps you identify where your organisation is most exposed.

**Buddy punching** is the most discussed form. One employee marks attendance for another - either by pressing their thumb on the biometric device or by logging in with their credentials on a digital system. The absent employee is recorded as present for some or all of the day. This is particularly common during late arrivals and early departures when supervisors are not watching the entrance.

**Extended breaks** are harder to detect because they are diffuse. An employee who takes 45-minute lunch breaks instead of the sanctioned 30 minutes loses 15 minutes per day. Over 250 working days, that is more than 62 hours - more than a week and a half of paid time per year.

**Early departure** often happens in combination with buddy punching: an employee marks their own exit on paper or requests a colleague to do it after they have already left the building.

**Personal activities on work time** - personal phone calls, running errands during work hours, spending time on personal tasks while appearing to work - fall into a grey zone. Some of this is unavoidable and normal. When it becomes habitual and significant, it crosses into time theft territory.

**Inflated overtime claims** occur when employees record OT hours they did not work or claim shift differentials they are not entitled to. This is most common in environments where overtime approval is informal and verification is weak.

## The Cost Calculation

Here is how to estimate what time theft is costing your organisation in concrete financial terms.

Take your average employee salary and divide it down to an hourly rate. For an employee earning ₹25,000 per month, the hourly rate on a standard 9-hour, 26-day month is approximately ₹107/hour.

Now estimate the average daily time loss. Studies across similar economies typically find this at 30–60 minutes per day across a workforce when buddy punching, extended breaks, and early departures are aggregated. Use a conservative 30 minutes for this exercise.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 100 employees | ₹25,000 | 30 minutes | ₹54 | ₹13,500 | ₹13,50,000 |
| 200 employees | ₹30,000 | 30 minutes | ₹64 | ₹16,000 | ₹32,00,000 |
| 500 employees | ₹35,000 | 30 minutes | ₹75 | ₹18,750 | ₹93,75,000 |

These numbers assume only 30 minutes per day - a figure that most HR professionals who have looked at this carefully consider conservative. When buddy punching is included, the effective loss per affected employee is often 1–2 hours per occurrence, not 30 minutes.

## Detection Without Software

For organisations that are not yet using automated attendance systems, there are still practical detection methods.

**Random supervisor spot-checks** during check-in and check-out windows are the simplest intervention. If employees know that a manager may be present at the attendance terminal, buddy punching drops significantly even without any technology change.

**Cross-referencing physical access logs** with attendance records can reveal discrepancies. If your building uses an access card for entry but attendance is recorded separately, compare the two datasets periodically.

**CCTV footage review** for suspected cases provides verification, but is time-intensive and should be used as an investigation tool rather than routine monitoring.

**Peer and supervisor reporting** works in some cultures but creates friction in others. Its effectiveness depends heavily on your organisation's trust environment.

The limitation of all manual detection methods is that they are reactive and inconsistent. They catch problems after they have already occurred and require significant HR or management bandwidth to execute.

## Prevention Strategies With Attendance Software

Automated attendance systems address time theft structurally rather than through policing.

**Biometric verification** eliminates buddy punching for in-person check-ins. A fingerprint or face cannot be delegated - only the actual employee can record their own attendance.

**Geo-tagged selfie check-in** for remote and field staff adds both location and facial verification, preventing an employee from checking in from home when they are supposed to be at a client site.

**Break time monitoring** through a mobile app or software-based check-in allows managers to see total hours present in a day, flagging cases where total productive time is consistently below the shift duration.

**Real-time dashboards** give managers immediate visibility into who has checked in, who is late, and who has not arrived - rather than discovering attendance gaps during payroll processing a month later.

**Automated exception alerts** flag specific patterns: an employee who consistently leaves 30 minutes before the end of shift, a team where check-out times cluster unnaturally around a single moment (suggesting buddy punching at departure), or a pattern of repeated late check-ins without manager-approved adjustments.

## Indian Labour Law and HR Considerations

Before implementing any surveillance-based measure, it is worth understanding the legal and HR context in India.

The Payment of Wages Act and Factories Act both require employers to maintain attendance records but do not prescribe the method. Biometric attendance is legally permissible and widely used.

If time theft is confirmed - particularly buddy punching - it typically constitutes misconduct under the Standing Orders of the establishment. Disciplinary proceedings can be initiated, but the process must follow natural justice principles: a show-cause notice, an opportunity for the employee to respond, and a proportionate penalty.

For first-time or minor offences, a written warning and documented counselling is usually appropriate. Termination is legally defensible only where there is documented evidence, proper disciplinary process, and where the offence is serious enough - typically involving deliberate fraud rather than extended breaks.

The strongest prevention approach combines technology (making time theft structurally difficult) with clear policy (so employees understand what is expected and what the consequences are) rather than relying on disciplinary action alone.

## Building a Culture That Reduces Time Theft

Technology and policy address the structural side. Culture addresses the motivational side.

Time theft typically increases in environments where employees feel disengaged, where they do not see a connection between their effort and outcomes, or where they perceive that the organisation is not treating them fairly. Addressing those root causes does more long-term good than any monitoring system.

This does not mean ignoring the systems problem. It means fixing both: deploy the systems that make fraud structurally harder, and simultaneously invest in the management practices that make employees want to give their actual effort to the organisation.
