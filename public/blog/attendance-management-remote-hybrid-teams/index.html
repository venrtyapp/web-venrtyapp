Attendance Management for Remote and Hybrid Teams: A Complete Guide for Indian Companies

April 10, 2026 7 min read read Ventryx Team

The pandemic forced Indian companies to figure out remote work in a matter of weeks. Most managed - poorly at first, then reasonably well. What many companies did not figure out in those two years of forced remote work was how to manage attendance for employees who are not physically present. The old biometric device at the office gate was suddenly irrelevant, and most HR teams either stopped tracking attendance formally or started relying on ad hoc solutions: email check-ins, WhatsApp confirmations, or the assumption that anyone who is responsive on Slack must be “at work.”

Now, with hybrid models becoming permanent at thousands of Indian companies, the attendance problem has gotten more complex, not less. You have some employees in office on some days, the same employees working from home on other days, and potentially field staff or remote-only hires in the mix. A single attendance policy no longer fits everyone, and a single technology solution probably does not either.

This guide covers the practical approaches that work, the policy decisions you actually need to make, and the legal grey areas Indian companies should understand before rolling out a remote attendance system.

Why Remote Attendance Is Harder Than It Looks

The challenge with remote attendance is not primarily technological. It is conceptual.

In an office, the question “is this person at work?” has a reasonably clear answer - they either walked through the door or they did not. In a remote context, the question becomes much fuzzier. Is someone “at work” when they are logged into their laptop? When they are on a call? When they are between tasks but available? When they are working at 10 PM because they took a long break in the afternoon?

This conceptual ambiguity leads to one of two failure modes:

Over-surveillance: Companies install invasive monitoring - keystroke logging, random screenshots, continuous GPS tracking, camera checks. This destroys trust, signals that the company does not respect its employees, and drives attrition among exactly the high-performers who have the most options.

Under-accountability: Companies give up on attendance tracking entirely for remote employees, relying on output and manager discretion. This works for senior knowledge workers but creates equity problems when the same employees have different accountability expectations than their office-based colleagues.

The answer lies between these extremes: presence verification that is proportionate, transparent, and tied to the actual working pattern rather than surveillance for its own sake.

Tracking Methods for Remote and Hybrid Employees

Method How It Works Best For Limitations
Mobile GPS check-in Employee opens app, records location at start and end of shift Field staff, delivery, site-based remote work Can be spoofed; needs data connection
Selfie + geo-tag check-in Photo + GPS captured at check-in Any remote employee; adds face verification Requires smartphone; minor friction
Calendar/shift-based login Employee confirms shift start in app; manager sets shift schedule Fixed-schedule remote employees Does not verify physical location
Project and task logging Time logged against tasks or tickets is used to derive attendance Software developers, knowledge workers Requires integration with project tool
VPN + system login tracking Attendance derived from system activity on the corporate network Technical teams with VPN usage Privacy concerns; does not work for all roles
Manager-verified check-in Employee messages manager to confirm availability; manager marks attendance Very small teams Not scalable; subjective

No single method works for every type of remote employee. The best approach is to assign a tracking method based on job role and working pattern, not apply one method uniformly to all remote staff.

Designing a WFH and Hybrid Attendance Policy

A workable policy needs to answer five questions clearly before it is communicated to employees.

1. What does “present” mean for a remote day?

Define this explicitly. Presence could mean: logged into the company system during core hours (e.g., 10 AM to 4 PM), available on communication tools and responsive within a defined window, or a check-in record via the attendance app at the start of the shift. Leave ambiguity out of this definition - it creates daily disputes.

2. What are the core hours, if any?

Many hybrid companies have moved to core hours (e.g., 11 AM to 3 PM) during which all employees, regardless of location, are expected to be available for synchronous communication. Outside core hours, flexibility is permitted. This model respects autonomy while ensuring collaboration.

3. How are late arrivals and partial days handled for remote employees?

The policy should be the same as for office employees - consistency is critical for fairness. If an office employee who arrives 45 minutes late has a documented late mark, a remote employee who checks in 45 minutes after their shift starts should be treated identically.

4. How many WFH days are permitted per week/month, and does this need pre-approval?

Hybrid policies vary widely. Some companies allow employees to choose which days they work from home with 24-hour advance notice. Others specify that certain days (e.g., Tuesday and Thursday) are mandated office days for all employees. Make the rule clear and enforce it consistently.

5. How is attendance recorded on WFH days, and where does it flow?

Employees need to know exactly what they are expected to do - open the app, log in, confirm availability - and managers need to know exactly where to see this data. If it requires HR to manually reconcile attendance registers at month-end, the system is not working.

Legal Grey Areas in India for Remote Work Attendance

India does not yet have a comprehensive remote work law. The IT/ITeS sector has operated under work-from-home provisions granted under state Shops Acts since the pandemic, but these provisions are not uniform and are subject to periodic renewal in some states.

Location of work matters more than employers typically realise. If an employee works from a state other than where they were hired, the applicable Shops and Establishments Act may change - affecting applicable leave rules, OT rates, and statutory deductions.

Monitoring and surveillance limits are not explicitly codified in Indian employment law, but employees do have rights under general contract principles and, increasingly, under data protection frameworks. Tracking an employee’s GPS location continuously throughout the day without disclosure and consent is a legal and reputational risk. Proportionate monitoring - a check-in at shift start and end, with location recorded only at those moments - is far more defensible.

Proof of work becomes relevant in disputes. If an employee claims they were working on a particular day and the company disputes it, what evidence does the company have? Attendance records alone may not be sufficient. A system that captures project activity, communication logs, or task completions alongside the check-in record provides much stronger documentation.

Recommendations by Team Type

Fully remote, knowledge work (software, analytics, content): Use project-based or task-based attendance tied to your project management tool, supplemented by a daily availability check-in on fixed core hours. Avoid GPS tracking - it serves no legitimate purpose for employees who have no location requirement.

Hybrid office/home mix: Use the office biometric system for office days and a selfie-based app check-in for home days. The attendance platform should unify both into a single record. Set a clear WFH day cap and pre-approval requirement.

Field staff with remote work components: Selfie-based geo-tagged check-in is the primary method. Add customer visit logging for site-based staff. Use manager-reviewed exception reports for patterns that suggest location fraud.

Customer-facing roles (support, sales) with WFH allowance: Schedule-based check-in tied to shift times, with availability confirmed through communication tool activity. Escalation procedures for unresponsive employees should be documented in policy.

Factory or industrial staff with partial WFH for administrative roles: Apply biometric for on-site days and calendar/app check-in for remote days. This category is rare but growing as companies allow administrative plant staff to work from home for non-floor activities.

Balancing Trust and Accountability

The companies that handle remote attendance best are not necessarily the ones with the most sophisticated tracking technology. They are the ones that have had an honest conversation with their teams about what accountability means in a distributed environment and built systems that reflect that conversation.

Employees generally accept reasonable check-ins when the purpose is clear - managing payroll accurately, ensuring fairness, and maintaining team coordination - and when the tracking is not more invasive than necessary. What they resist, reasonably, is being treated as suspects who need to be monitored to prove they are working.

A good remote attendance policy communicates: “We need to know when you are available and when you are not, for payroll and coordination purposes. We are not tracking everything you do. Here is exactly what gets recorded and why.”
